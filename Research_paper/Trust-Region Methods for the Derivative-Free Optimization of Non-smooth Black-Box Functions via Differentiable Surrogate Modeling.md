
## Abstract

We study the unconstrained minimization of a non-smooth, locally Lipschitz continuous black-box function $f_{\mathrm{NS}} : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ for which no derivative or sub-gradient information is available. Rather than building a non-smooth model of $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ directly — the route taken by Liuzzi, Lucidi, Rinaldi, and Vicente in their max-linear trust-region framework — we pursue a two-stage strategy: at each iteration we first construct a genuinely differentiable quadratic surrogate $f_\delta$ that approximates $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ to within a prescribed tolerance $\varepsilon$ on the current trust region, and we then solve a smooth trust-region subproblem on $f_\delta$ to produce the step. The surrogate is built from a well-poised interpolation set generated by Algorithm 6.4 of Conn, Scheinberg, and Vicente, and the subproblem is solved by a projected-gradient quadratic-box solver. We show that the trust-region radius $\Delta_k \to 0$ whenever $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ is bounded below, and, conditional on an assumption that the surrogate's approximation error $\varepsilon_k$ is driven to zero at least as fast as $\Delta_k$, we prove two partial, complementary Clarke-stationarity results rather than one unconditional one: along any limit point approached through directions that are asymptotically dense on the unit sphere, the limit is Clarke stationary (paralleling the reference paper's own density-based argument); and, independently of any density assumption, along any limit point where the surrogate's own gradient vanishes in the limit, the limit is Clarke stationary as well. The complementary case — a limit point reached with model gradients bounded away from zero along a non-dense sequence of directions — is identified explicitly and left open. The argument rests on a uniform-approximation lemma — for any finite sample set and any $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists a smooth function agreeing with $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ on that set while staying uniformly $\varepsilon$-close to it — combined with a radius-to-zero argument adapted from the convergence theory of trust-region derivative-free methods for non-smooth black-box functions. We relate the theory to four concrete step-selection rules implemented in the accompanying codebase (the convergence results above are proved only for one of the four) and report on the empirical trade-off between the model-based (bqmin) step and the raw interpolation-point step that motivated this line of analysis.

**Keywords.** non-smooth optimization, derivative-free optimization, trust-region methods, black-box functions, smooth surrogate modeling, Clarke generalized derivative

**AMS subject classifications.** 90C56, 90C30, 90C26

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Motivation

Consider the unconstrained minimization problem

$$
\min_{x \in \mathbb{R}^n} f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x), \tag{1}
$$

where $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ is treated as a pure black box: only function values are observable, no analytic form is known, and $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ is possibly nonsmooth without any assumption on where or how the nonsmoothness originates. This is precisely the setting studied by Liuzzi, Lucidi, Rinaldi, and Vicente in *Trust-Region Methods for the Derivative-Free Optimization of Nonsmooth Black-Box Functions* [LLRV19], hereafter referred to throughout as **the reference paper**. That paper's central contribution is the first convergent trust-region methodology for problem (1) under nonsmoothness of unknown origin. Its trust-region models are themselves nonsmooth: a max-linear term built from randomly generated, asymptotically dense directions, inspired by bundle methods, optionally summed with a quadratic term to exploit curvature in smooth subregions.

This paper studies a different route to the same goal, built around a design choice already present in the accompanying codebase (`general_model/`) before this analysis was undertaken: rather than assembling a nonsmooth max-linear model, build a **fully differentiable quadratic model** at every iteration, fit to a well-poised sample set by ordinary minimum-Frobenius-norm interpolation (Conn, Scheinberg, and Vicente's Algorithm 6.4 [CSV09, Alg. 6.4]), and solve the resulting *smooth* trust-region subproblem with a standard box-constrained quadratic solver. The intuition, recorded in the project's working notes and formalized below, is that a locally Lipschitz nonsmooth function can always be approximated to arbitrary accuracy on a bounded region by a smooth function agreeing with it on any prescribed finite point set — so that optimizing the smooth surrogate is, in an appropriate limit, equivalent to optimizing $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ itself. The purpose of this paper is to make that intuition precise, to state the assumptions under which it holds, and to prove a global convergence result — stationarity of limit points in the sense of the Clarke generalized derivative — that parallels the reference paper's Theorem 2.3 while following a genuinely different proof route: instead of arguing that a *max-linear* model captures the subdifferential through direction density alone, we argue that a *quadratic* model captures $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ itself, pointwise, to a tolerance that is driven to zero by the trust-region mechanism.

## 2. Notation, standing assumptions, and the two function classes

All norms are Euclidean unless stated otherwise. $B_\varepsilon(x) := \{y \in \mathbb{R}^n : \|y - x\| \le \varepsilon\}$ denotes the closed ball of radius $\varepsilon$ about $x$.

**Local Lipschitz continuity.** A function $\phi : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is *locally Lipschitz* at $x_0$ if there exist $K \ge 0$ and a neighborhood $E$ of $x_0$ such that

$$
\|\phi(x_1) - \phi(x_2)\| \le K \|x_1 - x_2\| \qquad \forall\, x_1, x_2 \in E. \tag{2}
$$

As in the reference paper, $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ is assumed locally Lipschitz continuous whenever the theory needs it, with no assumption on the origin of its nonsmoothness and no access to any element of its subdifferential.

**Clarke generalized derivative and subdifferential.** For $f$ Lipschitz continuous near $x$, and $d \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the Clarke generalized derivative [Cla83] is

$$
f^\circ(x; d) = \limsup_{y \to x,\, t \downarrow 0} \frac{f(y + td) - f(y)}{t},
$$

and the Clarke generalized subdifferential is

$$
\partial f(x) = \{\, \xi \in \mathbb{R}^n : f^\circ(x; v) \ge \xi^\top v \ \ \forall v \in \mathbb{R}^n \,\}.
$$

It is a standard fact that $f^\circ(x; d) = \max\{\xi^\top d : \xi \in \partial f(x)\}$. A point $x^\ast$ is *(first-order) Clarke stationary* for problem (1) when $f^\circ(x^\ast; d) \ge 0$ for all $d \in \mathbb{R}^n$.

**The two function classes in play.** Following the codebase's own separation, we work with two classes of objective:

- $f_{\mathrm{S}}$: a smooth (continuously differentiable, in practice twice continuously differentiable) function. This is the case implemented by `SmoothFunction` and serves as the control case in which the surrogate model is not merely an approximation but converges to the exact local Taylor model of $f_{\mathrm S}$ itself.
- $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$: a nonsmooth, locally Lipschitz function of unknown origin of nonsmoothness. This is the case implemented by `NonSmoothFunction` and is the object of the convergence theory in Section 4.

**Notation for the surrogate.** At iterate $x_k$ and radius $\varepsilon$ (which in the algorithm of Section 4 will be taken equal to the current trust-region radius $\Delta_k$), we let

$$
E_\varepsilon(x_k) := \Big\{\, f_\delta : B_\varepsilon(x_k) \to \mathbb{R} \ \Big|\ f_\delta \text{ differentiable, and } \forall x \in B_\varepsilon(x_k),\ \big| f_\delta(x) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x) \big| < \varepsilon \,\Big\} \tag{3}
$$

denote the set of differentiable functions that $\varepsilon$-uniformly approximate $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ on the ball of radius $\varepsilon$ about $x_k$.

*Remark on notation.* This is a direct formalization of the handwritten construction: "let $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ be the nonsmooth function of inspection... let $E$ be the set of [differentiable] functions passing through $x_0$ such that $\forall f_\delta \in E,\ \forall x \in B_\varepsilon(x_0),\ d(f_\delta(x), f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x)) < \varepsilon$." We use $d(\cdot,\cdot)$ and $|\cdot - \cdot|$ interchangeably for the (scalar) distance between function values, since $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ and $f_\delta$ are real-valued.

The set $E_\varepsilon(x_k)$ is nonempty for every $\varepsilon > 0$ whenever $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ is locally Lipschitz — this is exactly the content of the uniform-approximation lemma proved in Section 3 — but it is of course not a singleton, and it is not directly usable computationally, since membership in $E_\varepsilon(x_k)$ is a statement about all of $B_\varepsilon(x_k)$, which we cannot query exhaustively in a black-box setting. Section 3.2 sharpens the existence statement to one that additionally *passes through* a prescribed finite sample set, which is what makes the construction algorithmic.

## 3. A uniform smooth-approximation lemma

This section proves the theoretical core of the smoothing strategy: that a nonsmooth, locally Lipschitz function can always be sandwiched, on a bounded region and to arbitrary accuracy, by a differentiable function that additionally agrees exactly with $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ on any prescribed finite set of points. This is stronger than ordinary mollification (e.g., convolving with a smooth bump function), which achieves the uniform-approximation property but generally does *not* pass through prescribed data points — and passing through the sample points is what will let the surrogate be built by ordinary polynomial interpolation, matching the codebase's actual model builder.

### 3.1 Existence of a uniform smooth approximant

**Lemma 3.1 (uniform smoothability).** Let $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ be locally Lipschitz continuous on a bounded open set $E \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ with Lipschitz constant $K$. Then for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists a function $f_\delta \in C^1(E)$ such that

$$
\sup_{x \in E} \big| f_\delta(x) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x) \big| < \varepsilon. \tag{4}
$$

*Proof.* Let $\phi_\sigma$ be a standard $C^\infty$ mollifier: $\phi_\sigma \ge 0$, $\operatorname{supp}(\phi_\sigma) \subseteq B_\sigma(0)$, $\int \phi_\sigma = 1$. Since $E$ is bounded, extend $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ to $\mathbb{R}^n$ by a Lipschitz extension with the same constant $K$ (e.g., the McShane–Whitney extension $\tilde f(x) = \inf_{y \in E} \{f_{\mathrm{NS}}(y) + K\|x-y\|\}$, which is Lipschitz with constant $K$ and agrees with $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ on $E$). Define $f_\delta := \tilde f \ast \phi_\sigma$. Then $f_\delta \in C^\infty(\mathbb{R}^n) \subseteq C^1(E)$, and for every $x \in E$,

$$
|f_\delta(x) - \tilde f(x)| = \left| \int_{B_\sigma(0)} \big(\tilde f(x - y) - \tilde f(x)\big)\, \phi_\sigma(y)\, dy \right| \le \int_{B_\sigma(0)} K\|y\|\, \phi_\sigma(y)\, dy \le K\sigma,
$$

using the Lipschitz bound on $\tilde f$ and $\int \phi_\sigma = 1$. Choosing $\sigma < \varepsilon / K$ gives $\sup_{x \in E} |f_\delta(x) - \tilde f(x)| \le K\sigma < \varepsilon$, and since $\tilde f \equiv f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ on $E$, (4) follows. $\blacksquare$

This establishes $E_\varepsilon(x_0) \ne \emptyset$ in the notation of (3), for every $\varepsilon > 0$ and every center $x_0$ (take $E = B_\varepsilon(x_0)$ above, or any bounded open superset).

### 3.2 A uniform smooth approximant that interpolates a prescribed finite set

The mollified function constructed in Lemma 3.1 will not, in general, agree with $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ at any particular point — a real obstacle, because the algorithm of Section 4 needs the surrogate to be built *from data actually observed at the sample points*, not from an oracle mollification the black box does not give us access to. We now strengthen Lemma 3.1 to guarantee exact agreement with $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ on a prescribed finite point set while retaining the uniform $\varepsilon$-closeness everywhere else. This is exactly the second lemma in the handwritten construction: *"for non-smooth function $f$, $\forall \varepsilon > 0$ and $\forall$ finite set of points $\bar E$, $\exists$ a smooth function $f'$ passing through $\bar E$, s.t. $\forall x,\ d(f(x), f'(x)) < \varepsilon$."*

**Lemma 3.2** Let $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ be locally Lipschitz continuous on a bounded open set $E \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ with Lipschitz constant $K$, and let $\bar{E} = \{y^1, \dots, y^p\} \subset E$ be any finite set of points. Then for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists a function $f_\delta \in C^1(E)$ such that

$$
f_\delta(y^i) = f_{\mathrm{NS}}(y^i) \quad \text{for } i = 1, \dots, p, \qquad \text{and} \qquad \sup_{x \in E} \big| f_\delta(x) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x) \big| < \varepsilon. \tag{5}
$$

*Proof.* Let $\hat f$ be the mollified approximant from Lemma 3.1 built at tolerance $\varepsilon / 2$, so that $\sup_{x \in E} |\hat f(x) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x)| < \varepsilon/2$. In general $\hat f(y^i) \ne f_{\mathrm{NS}}(y^i)$. Define the pointwise defect $\delta^i := f_{\mathrm{NS}}(y^i) - \hat f(x)|_{x=y^i}$ for $i = 1, \dots, p$, and let $\{\psi_i\}_{i=1}^p \subset C^\infty(\mathbb{R}^n)$ be a set of $C^\infty$ bump functions with

$$
\psi_i(y^j) = \begin{cases} 1 & i = j \\ 0 & i \ne j \end{cases}, \qquad 0 \le \psi_i \le 1, \qquad \operatorname{supp}(\psi_i) \subset B_\rho(y^i),
$$

where $\rho > 0$ is chosen small enough that the balls $B_\rho(y^i)$ are pairwise disjoint (possible since $\bar E$ is finite) — this is a standard partition-of-unity-style construction, e.g. $\psi_i(x) = \chi\big(\|x - y^i\| / \rho\big)$ for a fixed smooth cutoff $\chi$ with $\chi(0)=1$, $\chi \equiv 0$ outside $[0,1)$. Define

$$
f_\delta(x) := \hat f(x) + \sum_{i=1}^p \delta^i\, \psi_i(x). \tag{6}
$$

Then $f_\delta \in C^\infty(\mathbb{R}^n) \subseteq C^1(E)$ as a finite sum of smooth functions. At each sample point, since the $\psi_i$ have disjoint support, $f_\delta(y^j) = \hat f(y^j) + \delta^j = f_{\mathrm{NS}}(y^j)$, giving the interpolation property in (5). For the uniform bound, on the complement of $\bigcup_i B_\rho(y^i)$ every $\psi_i \equiv 0$ so $f_\delta = \hat f$ there and $|f_\delta - f_{\mathrm{NS}}| < \varepsilon/2 < \varepsilon$ directly. Inside $B_\rho(y^i)$, at most one bump is active (disjoint supports) and

$$
|f_\delta(x) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x)| \le |\hat f(x) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x)| + |\delta^i|\,\psi_i(x) \le \frac{\varepsilon}{2} + |\delta^i|.
$$

By definition $|\delta^i| = |f_{\mathrm{NS}}(y^i) - \hat f(y^i)| < \varepsilon/2$ from Lemma 3.1's bound applied at $x = y^i$. Hence $|f_\delta(x) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x)| < \varepsilon/2 + \varepsilon/2 = \varepsilon$ throughout $B_\rho(y^i)$ as well, and (5) holds on all of $E$. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary 3.3.** Under the hypotheses of Lemma 3.2, for every $x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n$, every $\varepsilon > 0$, and every finite $\bar{E} \subset B_\varepsilon(x_0)$, the set $E_\varepsilon(x_0)$ defined in (3) contains a function that additionally interpolates $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ exactly on $\bar E$.

This corollary is what licenses the construction used in Section 4: at every iteration $k$, the sample set is exactly the finite set $\bar E = \{y^1_k, \dots, y^p_k\}$ produced by the interpolation-set builder (Algorithm 6.4), and Corollary 3.3 guarantees that *some* smooth function passing through the observed values $f_{\mathrm{NS}}(y^i_k)$ stays uniformly within $\varepsilon$ of $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ on the whole trust region — even though the algorithm itself, being derivative-free, has no way to construct that particular witness function and instead builds the computationally tractable quadratic interpolant of Section 4.1. The role of Lemma 3.2 is purely existential: it certifies that the *target* the quadratic interpolant is chasing — "a smooth function agreeing with the sampled data, uniformly close to $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$" — is a nonempty, achievable target, not a contradiction in terms. The gap between "some interpolating smooth function is $\varepsilon$-close" and "the particular quadratic interpolant fitted by `fitfroquad` is $\varepsilon$-close" is exactly where Assumption 4.2 in the next section enters: rather than proving the quadratic model's approximation error directly (a classical but separate line of argument in interpolation-based derivative-free optimization, resting on the poisedness of the sample set — see Conn, Scheinberg, and Vicente [CSV09, Ch. 3–6]), we *posit* that the trust-region mechanism forces this error to zero and prove convergence conditional on that assumption, exactly mirroring how the reference paper's own Assumption 2.1 posits a bound on the model Hessian rather than deriving one.

## 4. Smoothed-DFO-TRNS: algorithm and convergence

### 4.1 The smooth trust-region model

At iteration $k$, with current iterate $x_k$ and trust-region radius $\Delta_k$, the algorithm builds a differentiable quadratic model

$$
m_k(s) = f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k) + g_k^\top s + \tfrac{1}{2} s^\top B_k s, \tag{7}
$$

exactly the smooth quadratic form used in the reference paper's Basic DFO-TRNS model (2), but here $g_k$ and $B_k$ are **not** randomly generated: they are the gradient and Hessian, at $x_k$, of a quadratic function interpolating $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ on a poised sample set $Y_k = \{x_k + d^1_k, \dots, x_k + d^p_k\} \subset B_{\gamma \Delta_k}(x_k)$, with $p = (n+1)(n+2)/2$ and $\gamma \ge 1$ a geometry constant. We borrow this containment condition *by analogy* from the reference paper's own condition (32), $\|y_k^j - x_k\| \le \gamma\Delta_k$ — worth flagging precisely, since the two conditions constrain different objects: the reference paper's (32) governs the bundle-style auxiliary points $\{y_k^j\}$ used to build the *nonsmooth max-linear term* of its Advanced DFO-TRNS, not a quadratic-interpolation sample set; the reference paper's own quadratic-term sample set (analogous in role to our $Y_k$) is instead described in its Section 4 as built from minimum-Frobenius-norm models following [CSV09, Sec. 5], starting from $2n+1$ points and growing to $(n+1)(n+2)/2$. The functional form of the containment condition is identical and the analogy is apt, but the two conditions are not literally the same statement about the same set. Concretely, and matching `algorithm_6_4` / `fitfroquad` in the accompanying implementation:

1. **Sample-set construction.** $Y_k$ is built by Algorithm 6.4 of Conn, Scheinberg, and Vicente [CSV09], which greedily selects offsets $d^i_k$ to maximize a Lagrange-polynomial pivot value, guaranteeing $\Lambda$-poisedness of $Y_k$ in $B_{\Delta_k}(x_k)$ for a constant $\Lambda$ independent of $k$ (this poisedness constant is the standing geometric assumption under which interpolation-based DFO theory operates; we do not re-derive it here and simply cite it as a property of the sample-set builder — see [CSV09, Thm. 3.14]).
2. **Black-box evaluation.** $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ is evaluated at every point of $Y_k$ (and at $x_k$ itself), which is the *only* information the algorithm extracts from the black box at this step — no gradients, no subgradients, nothing beyond function values, matching the pure black-box premise of problem (1).
3. **Interpolation fit.** The unique (if $p = (n+1)(n+2)/2$ and $Y_k$ is poised) or minimum-Frobenius-norm (if $Y_k$ is smaller and under-determined) quadratic $q_k(s) = c_k + g_k^\top s + \tfrac{1}{2} s^\top B_k s$ satisfying $q_k(d^i_k) = f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k + d^i_k)$ for all $i$ is computed by ordinary least squares against the monomial basis, exactly as implemented in `fitfroquad`. We write $f_\delta^{(k)}(x_k + s) := q_k(s)$ for this quadratic, viewed as a local surrogate for $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ on $B_{\Delta_k}(x_k)$.

By construction $f_\delta^{(k)}(y^i) = f_{\mathrm{NS}}(y^i)$ for every $y^i \in Y_k$: the surrogate interpolates the observed data exactly, matching the *interpolating* smooth approximant guaranteed to exist by Corollary 3.3. What Corollary 3.3 does **not** by itself guarantee is that this *particular* interpolant — the low-degree quadratic singled out by the poisedness-based fitting procedure, rather than an arbitrary smooth interpolant — is uniformly close to $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ off the sample points; this is the content of Assumption 4.2 below.

### 4.2 The trust-region subproblem and the algorithm

The trust-region subproblem solved at each iteration is

$$
s_k \in \operatorname*{argmin}_{\|s\|_\infty \le \Delta_k} m_k(s), \tag{8}
$$

an $\ell_\infty$-ball (box) variant of the reference paper's $\ell_2$-ball subproblem (2), matching the `bqmin` solver actually used (`bqmin(h, g, -\Delta_k \mathbf 1, \Delta_k \mathbf 1)` — note that in the solver's own signature `bqmin(A, B, Low, Upp)` minimizes $\tfrac12 X^\top A X + B^\top X$, so the second argument `B` there is a *gradient vector*, not a Hessian; we write $(g_k,B_k)$ for $(\text{gradient},\text{Hessian})$ throughout this paper and note the letter clash with the solver's own parameter name only here, to avoid any reader confusing `bqmin`'s `B` with this paper's $B_k$). This is an inessential difference from the reference paper — the reference paper's convergence proof uses the $\ell_2$ ball only insofar as $\|s_k\|^2 \le \Delta_k^2$ and $\|g_k\| = 1$ enter the KKT conditions and the Sherman–Morrison–Woodbury step; the analogous KKT system for the box-constrained subproblem (8) yields the same qualitative conclusion (an unconstrained stationary point when it lies in the box, an active-bound solution otherwise). Proposition 4.7 below is stated and proved for the $\ell_2$-ball case for direct comparability with the reference paper's Proposition 2.1, with a remark immediately following its proof that carries the same conclusion over to the $\ell_\infty$-box subproblem (8) actually solved by `bqmin`.

$$
\rho_k = \frac{f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k + s_k)}{\theta \|s_k\|^{1+p_0}}, \tag{9}
$$

using $p_0 > 0$ for the forcing-function exponent to avoid clashing with the sample-set cardinality $p$ used above (the reference paper uses the same symbol $p$ for both; we disambiguate here only because both quantities appear in the same section).

**Algorithm Smoothed-DFO-TRNS.**

> **Initialization.** Select $x_0 \in \mathbb{R}^n$, $\eta_1, \theta > 0$, $0 < \gamma_1 < 1 \le \gamma_2$, $\Delta_0 > 0$, $p_0 > 0$.
> **For** $k = 0, 1, \dots$
> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Build the poised sample set $Y_k \subset B_{\gamma \Delta_k}(x_k)$ via Algorithm 6.4; evaluate $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ on $Y_k \cup \{x_k\}$.
> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Fit the interpolating quadratic $f_\delta^{(k)}$, obtaining $(g_k, B_k)$ via `fitfroquad`.
> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Let $s_k$ solve subproblem (8).
> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;Compute $\rho_k$ by (9).
> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;**If** $\rho_k \ge \eta_1$ **then** set `SUCCESS` $\leftarrow$ **true**, $x_{k+1} \leftarrow x_k + s_k$, $\Delta_{k+1} \leftarrow \gamma_2 \Delta_k$.
> &nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;&nbsp;**Else** set `SUCCESS` $\leftarrow$ **false**, $x_{k+1} \leftarrow x_k$, $\Delta_{k+1} \leftarrow \gamma_1 \Delta_k$.
> **End For**

This is the `method = 0` (bqmin-step) variant of `trust_region_optimization_function` in the accompanying implementation; Section 5 discusses the other three implemented variants and how the convergence theory below does or does not extend to them.

### 4.3 Standing assumptions

**Assumption 4.1 (boundedness of the model Hessian).** There exist $q \in (0,1)$, $m, M > 0$, such that the maximal eigenvalue of $B_k$ satisfies $\lambda_{\max}(B_k) \le M \Delta_k^{-q}$, and whenever $B_k$ has negative eigenvalues, $-\lambda_{\min}(B_k) \le m \Delta_k^{-q}$.

This is verbatim the reference paper's Assumption 2.1. It is not automatic for an interpolation-fitted Hessian and must be imposed as a standing hypothesis here exactly as it is there; unlike the reference paper's setting, where $B_k$ is a free modeling choice bounded only by this assumption, here $B_k$ is *determined* by the interpolation data, so Assumption 4.1 is a genuine restriction on the class of $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ (or rather, on the growth of its finite-difference curvature as sampled by the poised sets) admitted by the theory, not a design freedom. We flag this honestly as the least satisfying assumption in this paper and return to it in Section 6.

**Assumption 4.2 (vanishing surrogate error).** There exists a sequence $\varepsilon_k \to 0$ such that

$$
\sup_{x \in B_{\Delta_k}(x_k)} \big| f_\delta^{(k)}(x) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x) \big| \le \varepsilon_k \qquad \text{for all } k. \tag{10}
$$

This is the assumption that converts Corollary 3.3's *existence* statement into a property of the *specific* interpolating quadratic actually computed by the algorithm. It is the direct formalization of the handwritten note "since $\varepsilon_0 \to 0$... we have the approximate local min of $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$." We do not derive Assumption 4.2 from first principles in this paper (doing so rigorously requires a quantitative Taylor-remainder bound for $\Lambda$-poised quadratic interpolation controlled by higher-order smoothness of $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ restricted to $B_{\Delta_k}(x_k)$, which does not exist in general for a merely Lipschitz $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ — see the discussion in Section 6). Instead, Lemma 4.5 below shows the one fact we *can* prove unconditionally: $\Delta_k \to 0$, which is the mechanism that makes Assumption 4.2 plausible (a shrinking ball is easier to approximate uniformly, since local Lipschitz variation on it is bounded by $K \cdot \operatorname{diam} = 2K\Delta_k \to 0$) even though it does not by itself prove (10) for the specific quadratic interpolant used.

**Assumption 4.3 (function bounded below).** $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ is bounded below on $\mathbb{R}^n$.

**Assumption 4.4 (asymptotic density of model gradients along limiting subsequences).** For any subsequence $K$ along which $x_k \to x^\ast$ and $\Delta_{k+1} < \Delta_k$, the set of unit vectors $\{g_k / \|g_k\|\}_K$ is dense in the unit sphere.

Assumption 4.4 replaces the reference paper's built-in random generation of $g_k$, which makes density essentially free (the reference paper only needs to observe that randomly, densely generated directions remain dense along any subsequence). In our setting $g_k$ is the gradient of an interpolation model and is *not* randomly generated, so density is not automatic and must be assumed; Section 4.4 discusses what this assumption means geometrically and how the four solver variants of Section 5 interact with it.

### 4.4 Convergence analysis

**Lemma 4.5 (radius goes to zero).** *Suppose Assumption 4.3 holds and let Assumption 4.1 hold. Any sequence $\{\Delta_k\}$ of trust-region radii produced by Algorithm Smoothed-DFO-TRNS is such that $\lim_{k \to \infty} \Delta_k = 0$.*

*Proof.* This is the reference paper's Lemma 2.1, and the proof is identical because it uses only the acceptance test (9) and Assumption 4.1 — it never touches the origin of $g_k$ or $B_k$. We reproduce it for completeness. Suppose, by contradiction, that $\{\Delta_k\}$ does not converge to zero. Then there exists $\epsilon > 0$ such that $\#\{k : \Delta_k > \epsilon\} = \infty$, and because $\Delta_k$ only shrinks on unsuccessful iterations, there must be infinitely many $k$ with $\Delta_k > \epsilon$ and $\Delta_{k+1} \ge \Delta_k$, i.e., infinitely many successful iterations ($\rho_k \ge \eta_1$) with $\Delta_k > \epsilon$.

If $\lambda_{\min}(B_k) \le 0$, standard trust-region subproblem theory [DS83, §6.4.2] gives $\|s_k\| = \Delta_k$. Otherwise, either $\|s_k\| = \Delta_k$ or $s_k = -B_k^{-1} g_k$; in the latter case Assumption 4.1 gives $\|B_k^{-1} g_k\| \ge \lambda_{\min}(B_k^{-1})^{-1}$-type bounds identical to the reference paper's derivation, yielding $\|B_k^{-1} g_k\| \ge (1/M) \Delta_k^q \|g_k\|$. Combining both cases and unwinding the acceptance test (9),

$$
f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k + s_k) \ge \eta_1 \theta \|s_k\|^{1+p_0} \ge \eta_1 \theta \min\{\|B_k^{-1} g_k\|, \Delta_k\}^{1+p_0} \ge \eta_1 \theta \min\Big\{\tfrac{\epsilon^q}{M}\|g_k\|, \epsilon\Big\}^{1+p_0}.
$$

Since this holds for infinitely many $k$, and $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ is reduced by at least a fixed positive constant infinitely often while being bounded below (Assumption 4.3), we obtain a contradiction. $\blacksquare$

*Remark.* One subtlety not present in the reference paper's version of this proof: there, $\|g_k\| = 1$ is guaranteed by construction (unit-sphere generation), so the bound $\min\{\epsilon^q/M, \epsilon\}^{1+p_0}$ is an honest positive constant. Here $g_k$ is an interpolation gradient of a priori unknown norm. If $\|g_k\| \to 0$ along the relevant subsequence the bound above degenerates — the reduction $f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k+s_k)$ is still nonnegative and the contradiction argument for $\Delta_k\to 0$ still holds (it only needed *infinitely many* $\Delta_k>\epsilon$ successful iterations to be impossible under boundedness below, which remains true regardless of how the per-iteration reduction bound scales with $\|g_k\|$), so Lemma 4.5's conclusion is unaffected; what changes is only that $g_k\to0$ marks exactly the regime handled by Theorem 4.10 below (via the vanishing-model-gradient route) rather than by Theorem 4.9 (the direction-density route, which implicitly needs $g_k\ne0$ to normalize).

**Property 4.6 (steps track the negative model gradient).** *Any sequence $\{(x_k, s_k, \Delta_k)\}$ generated by Algorithm Smoothed-DFO-TRNS is such that $s_k = -\Delta_k D_k g_k$ with $D_k \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ satisfying $\lim_{k \to \infty} D_k = I$.*

**Proposition 4.7.** *Let Assumption 4.1 hold. Assume also that all trust-region subproblems (8) are solved up to optimality. Then Algorithm Smoothed-DFO-TRNS generates sequences $\{(x_k, s_k, \Delta_k)\}$ satisfying Property 4.6, for $k$ sufficiently large.*

*Proof.* This is the reference paper's Proposition 2.1, adapted to a general (not necessarily unit) $g_k$. The Sherman–Morrison–Woodbury half of the argument — from the KKT system (3)/(4)-type identity through the formula for $D_k$ — is purely algebraic and transfers without any change, so we do not repeat it. The part that genuinely needs re-derivation, and that an earlier draft of this proof glossed over by simply substituting $\|g_k\|$ for $1$ into the final bound, is the derivation of the bounds on $\eta_k := -s_k^\top B_k s_k - g_k^\top s_k$ in the first place, because the reference paper's upper bound on $\eta_k$ comes from comparing the subproblem's optimal value against the value at a *specific feasible comparator point*, and that comparator point is only feasible — only lies on the boundary $\|s\|=\Delta_k$ — because $\|g_k\|=1$ there. We redo this step explicitly.

Since $s_k$ solves subproblem (8) — for this comparison we use the $\ell_2$-ball form $\|s\|^2\le\Delta_k^2$, matching the reference paper's subproblem (2), and return to the box case in the remark following this proof — $s_k$ attains a model value no worse than at any other feasible point. For a general (nonzero) $g_k$, the boundary point in the direction $-g_k$ is $-\Delta_k\, g_k/\|g_k\|$, not $-\Delta_k g_k$; the latter is only on the boundary when $\|g_k\|=1$, which is exactly the fact the earlier draft's "identical argument" claim implicitly needed and did not check. Using the correctly rescaled comparator,

$$
f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k) + g_k^\top s_k + \tfrac12 s_k^\top B_k s_k \ \le\ f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k) + g_k^\top\Big(-\Delta_k\frac{g_k}{\|g_k\|}\Big) + \tfrac12 \Delta_k^2 \frac{g_k^\top B_k g_k}{\|g_k\|^2},
$$

i.e.

$$
\tfrac12 s_k^\top B_k s_k + g_k^\top s_k \ \le\ \tfrac12 \Delta_k^2 \lambda_{\max}(B_k) - \Delta_k \|g_k\|, \tag{5$'$}
$$

which is the direct analogue of the reference paper's inequality (5), with $g_k^\top g_k = \|g_k\|^2$ (not $1$) now appearing on the right rather than being absorbed as $1$. Recalling $\eta_k = -2\big(\tfrac12 s_k^\top B_k s_k + g_k^\top s_k\big) + g_k^\top s_k$ and applying Assumption 4.1 to (5$'$) exactly as the reference paper does to its (5), we obtain the lower bound

$$
\eta_k \ \ge\ -\Delta_k^2\lambda_{\max}(B_k) + 2\Delta_k\|g_k\| + g_k^\top s_k \ \ge\ -M\Delta_k^{2-q} + 2\Delta_k\|g_k\| - \Delta_k\|g_k\| \ =\ -M\Delta_k^{2-q} + \Delta_k\|g_k\|, \tag{6$'$}
$$

using $|g_k^\top s_k| \le \|g_k\|\,\|s_k\| \le \Delta_k\|g_k\|$ in place of the reference paper's $\|g_k\|=1,\|s_k\|\le\Delta_k \Rightarrow |g_k^\top s_k|\le\Delta_k$. This is the correctly re-derived, not merely reformatted, generalization of the reference paper's (6), and it guarantees $\eta_k \ne 0$ for $k$ large exactly as before, now legitimately (the rescaled comparator point is what makes (5$'$) valid in the first place). The matching upper bound on $\eta_k$ (the reference paper's (7)) uses only $\|s_k\|\le\Delta_k$ and Assumption 4.1, neither of which references $\|g_k\|$, so it carries over unchanged: $\eta_k \le m\Delta_k^{-q}\Delta_k^2 + \Delta_k\|g_k\| = m\Delta_k^{2-q} + \Delta_k\|g_k\|$.

Dividing (6$'$) and its matching upper bound by $\Delta_k\|g_k\|$ (valid once $g_k\ne0$, which holds along any subsequence with $\|g_k\|\not\to0$ — the case of interest for Theorem 4.9; the complementary case $g_k\to0$ is handled separately by Theorem 4.10 and does not need Property 4.6 at all, since there the conclusion is reached without reference to step direction) gives $-M\Delta_k^{1-q}/\|g_k\| + 1 \le \eta_k/(\Delta_k\|g_k\|) \le 1 + m\Delta_k^{1-q}/\|g_k\|$, so that $\eta_k/(\Delta_k\|g_k\|)\to1$ whenever $\|g_k\|$ is bounded away from $0$ along the subsequence in question — which is precisely the non-degeneracy condition under which Property 4.6 is invoked in the proof of Theorem 4.9. From here the Sherman–Morrison–Woodbury algebra proceeds exactly as in the reference paper's derivation of its (8)–(9), now with $g_k$ (not a unit vector) in every slot, giving $D_k \to I$. $\blacksquare$

*Remark (the $\ell_2$ vs.\ $\ell_\infty$ trust region used in the comparator step).* The comparison above was carried out for the $\ell_2$-ball subproblem to match the reference paper's derivation term for term. The implementation actually solves the $\ell_\infty$-box subproblem (8) via `bqmin`. The comparator point used above, $-\Delta_k g_k/\|g_k\|$, lies on the $\ell_2$ boundary but only inside (not on) the $\ell_\infty$ box $\|s\|_\infty\le\Delta_k$ whenever $g_k$ has more than one nonzero coordinate — so for the box subproblem the sharp comparator is instead the scaled point $-\Delta_k\, g_k/\|g_k\|_\infty$, which lies exactly on the box boundary. Repeating (5$'$)–(6$'$) with $\|g_k\|_\infty$ in place of $\|g_k\|$ throughout gives the same qualitative conclusion ($\eta_k/(\Delta_k\|g_k\|_\infty)\to1$, hence $D_k\to I$) since $\|\cdot\|_\infty$ and $\|\cdot\|_2$ are equivalent norms on $\mathbb{R}^n$ (differing by at most a factor of $\sqrt n$, which does not affect any of the limits taken above). We state the $\ell_2$ version in the main proof only because it is what lets the algebra be checked line-by-line against the reference paper's own equations; the box-subproblem version is the one that actually applies to the implemented solver and holds by the same argument with this one substitution.

**Lemma 4.8 (limiting model gradient stationarity is inherited by $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$).** *Suppose Assumptions 4.1–4.3 hold. Let $\{(x_k, s_k, \Delta_k)\}$ be a sequence generated by Algorithm Smoothed-DFO-TRNS. Let $L \subseteq K = \{k : \Delta_{k+1} < \Delta_k\}$ be an index set such that*

$$
\lim_{k \in L, k \to \infty} x_k = x^\ast \qquad \text{and} \qquad \lim_{k \in L, k \to \infty} \frac{s_k}{\|s_k\|} = s^\ast.
$$

*Then $f^\circ(x^\ast; s^\ast) \ge 0$.*

*Proof.* The reference paper's Lemma 2.2 proves this same conclusion for the true function $f$ directly from the unsuccess of iterations in $L$ ($\rho_k < \eta_1$) and the Lipschitz continuity of $f$ near $x^\ast$ — it never uses the model at all beyond the acceptance-test inequality, which is stated purely in terms of $f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k)$ and $f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k+s_k)$, both black-box quantities available regardless of how the model was built. Concretely, for each $k \in L$, unsuccess gives

$$
f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k + s_k) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k) > -\eta_1 \theta \|s_k\|^{1+p_0},
$$

from which, dividing by $\|s_k\|$,

$$
\frac{f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k + \|s_k\|[s_k/\|s_k\|]) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k)}{\|s_k\|} > -\eta_1 \theta \|s_k\|^{p_0}. \tag{11}
$$

Introducing $s^\ast$ into the quotient of (11) exactly as in the reference paper — via the intermediate quantity $\frac{f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k + \|s_k\|s^\ast) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k)}{\|s_k\|}$ and bounding the difference between this and the left side of (11) by $L_f^\ast \|[s_k/\|s_k\|] - s^\ast\|$, where $L_f^\ast$ is the local Lipschitz constant of $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ near $x^\ast$ — and using Lemma 4.5 ($\|s_k\| \to 0$ since $\Delta_k \to 0$ and $\|s_k\| \le \gamma\Delta_k$) and the hypothesis $s_k/\|s_k\| \to s^\ast$, we obtain

$$
f^\circ(x^\ast; s^\ast) \ge \limsup_{k \in L, k \to \infty} \frac{f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k + \|s_k\|s^\ast) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k)}{\|s_k\|} \ge 0,
$$

which is exactly the chain of inequalities (10)–(13) of the reference paper, with $f$ replaced throughout by $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$; no other change is needed since this step of the argument never involves the model. $\blacksquare$

We now reach the point where our proof genuinely departs from the reference paper's. The reference paper concludes stationarity of $x^\ast$ in one further step (its Theorem 2.3), invoking density of $\{g_k\}_K$ in the unit sphere — a property it gets for free because $g_k$ is *randomly generated to be dense by construction*, independent of the function being optimized. In our setting $g_k$ is the interpolation gradient of the surrogate model, and its distribution over the unit sphere along a convergent subsequence is a property of $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ and the sampling geometry, not a designed-in guarantee. We therefore must either assume density directly (Assumption 4.4) or derive stationarity through a different mechanism specific to the fact that $g_k$ is an actual gradient of a function approximating $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$, rather than an arbitrary direction. We give both routes, since they serve different purposes: Theorem 4.9 is the direct analogue of the reference paper's Theorem 2.3 (density-based, structurally parallel, but honest about resting on Assumption 4.4); Theorem 4.10 is the route native to the smoothing idea, using Assumption 4.2 instead of density, and is the theorem that most faithfully captures "optimize the smooth surrogate to optimize $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$."

**Theorem 4.9 (stationarity via direction density — direct analogue of the reference paper's Theorem 2.3).** *Suppose Assumptions 4.1, 4.3, and 4.4 hold. Let Algorithm Smoothed-DFO-TRNS satisfy Property 4.6. Let $x^\ast$ be any limit point of $\{x_k\}$ and $K \subseteq \{k : \Delta_{k+1} < \Delta_k\}$ a subset of indices such that $\lim_{k \in K, k \to \infty} x_k = x^\ast$. If the subsequence $\{g_k\}_K$ (normalized) is dense in the unit sphere, then $x^\ast$ is Clarke stationary for problem (1).*

*Proof.* Identical in structure to the reference paper's Theorem 2.3 proof, substituting Lemma 4.8 for its Lemma 2.2 and Property 4.6 for its Property 2.1: suppose for contradiction $x^\ast$ is not stationary, so some unit $\bar g$ has $f^\circ(x^\ast; -\bar g) < 0$. Density of $\{g_k/\|g_k\|\}_K$ lets us extract a further subsequence (still called $K$) with $x_k \to x^\ast$, $g_k / \|g_k\| \to \bar g$, and $\Delta_k \to 0$. Property 4.6 gives $s_k / \|s_k\| \to -\bar g$ along this subsequence (since $D_k \to I$ and $s_k = -\Delta_k D_k g_k$ points, up to normalization, along $-g_k/\|g_k\| \to -\bar g$). Lemma 4.8 then gives $f^\circ(x^\ast; -\bar g) \ge 0$, a contradiction. $\blacksquare$

**Assumption 4.2$'$ (rate-strengthened vanishing surrogate error).** In addition to Assumption 4.2, $\varepsilon_k = o(\Delta_k)$, i.e., the surrogate error vanishes strictly faster than the trust-region radius itself, not merely alongside it. This is the quantitative reading of "$\varepsilon_0 \to 0$" in the handwritten construction, and it costs nothing in the *existence* theory of Section 3: the mollification parameter $\sigma$ in Lemma 3.1 is a free choice at every iteration, so nothing prevents choosing $\sigma_k = o(\Delta_k)$ there. It is stated as a separate, explicitly labeled assumption — rather than folded silently into Assumption 4.2 — precisely so that Theorem 4.10 below can cite exactly the hypotheses its proof uses, no more and no less.

**Theorem 4.10 (stationarity via vanishing surrogate error — the route native to smoothing).** *Suppose Assumptions 4.1, 4.2$'$, and 4.3 hold. Let $x^\ast$ be any limit point of $\{x_k\}$ and $K \subseteq \{k : \Delta_{k+1} < \Delta_k\}$ a subset of indices such that $\lim_{k \in K, k \to \infty} x_k = x^\ast$. Suppose in addition that along $K$ the model gradients satisfy $\liminf_{k \in K} \|g_k\| = 0$. Then $x^\ast$ is Clarke stationary for problem (1).*

*Proof.* Pass to a further subsequence, still indexed by $K$, with $\|g_k\| \to 0$. We first show that $x^\ast$ is a limit of approximate stationary points of $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ in the following precise sense, then upgrade to exact Clarke stationarity.

*Step 1 — an explicit (not merely little-$o$) remainder bound.* Fix $k \in K$. By Assumption 4.2, $|f_\delta^{(k)}(y) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(y)| \le \varepsilon_k$ for every $y \in B_{\Delta_k}(x_k)$, and $f_\delta^{(k)}(x_k) = f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k)$ since $x_k$ is itself an evaluated interpolation node. Unlike a generic differentiable function, $f_\delta^{(k)}$ is by construction the *exact quadratic* $f_\delta^{(k)}(x_k+s) = f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k) + g_k^\top s + \tfrac12 s^\top B_k s$, so its Taylor remainder is not an abstract $o(t)$ term but the quadratic form itself, exactly:

$$
f_\delta^{(k)}(x_k + td) - f_\delta^{(k)}(x_k) = t\, g_k^\top d + \frac{t^2}{2}\, d^\top B_k d \qquad \text{for every } t \in \mathbb{R},\ \|d\|=1. \tag{12}
$$

Assumption 4.1 bounds this remainder explicitly: $|d^\top B_k d| \le \max\{\lambda_{\max}(B_k), -\lambda_{\min}(B_k)\} \le \max\{M,m\}\, \Delta_k^{-q} =: C\Delta_k^{-q}$. Combining (12) with the uniform surrogate bound exactly as before,

$$
f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k+td) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k) \ge f_\delta^{(k)}(x_k+td) - \varepsilon_k - f_\delta^{(k)}(x_k) = t\,g_k^\top d + \frac{t^2}{2}d^\top B_k d - \varepsilon_k,
$$

valid for every $t \in (0, \Delta_k]$ (so that $x_k + td \in B_{\Delta_k}(x_k)$). Dividing by $t$ and applying the explicit bound on the quadratic term,

$$
\frac{f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k+td) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k)}{t} \ge g_k^\top d - \frac{\varepsilon_k}{t} - \frac{t\, C}{2}\,\Delta_k^{-q}. \tag{13}
$$

Every term on the right of (13) is now written explicitly in $k, t, d$, with no term whose vanishing is merely asserted rather than checked; this is what makes the limit taken in Step 2 verifiable.

*Step 2 — a step-size schedule that dominates both error terms.* We need $t_k \in (0, \Delta_k]$ with $t_k \to 0$ while simultaneously $\varepsilon_k/t_k \to 0$ (to kill the surrogate-error term) and $t_k \Delta_k^{-q} \to 0$ (to kill the curvature term). Define

$$
t_k := \min\big\{\, \Delta_k,\ \sqrt{\varepsilon_k\, \Delta_k^{\,q}} \,\big\}. \tag{14}
$$

The truncation by $\Delta_k$ makes $t_k \in (0, \Delta_k]$ automatic (for $k$ large enough that $\varepsilon_k, \Delta_k > 0$, which holds eventually), so it only remains to check that both error terms vanish along this $t_k$, which we do by bounding each above by the corresponding quantity computed from the untruncated candidate $\hat t_k := \sqrt{\varepsilon_k \Delta_k^q}$, since $t_k \le \hat t_k$ makes $\varepsilon_k/t_k \ge \varepsilon_k/\hat t_k$ so this direction needs separate care, while $t_k\Delta_k^{-q}\le\hat t_k\Delta_k^{-q}$ transfers directly; we treat the two terms in turn.

For the curvature term, $t_k \le \hat t_k$ gives directly $t_k \Delta_k^{-q} \le \hat t_k \Delta_k^{-q} = \sqrt{\varepsilon_k \Delta_k^{\,q}}\cdot \Delta_k^{-q} = \sqrt{\varepsilon_k \Delta_k^{-q}} = \sqrt{(\varepsilon_k/\Delta_k)\cdot \Delta_k^{1+q}}$. By Assumption 4.2$'$, $\varepsilon_k/\Delta_k \to 0$; since $\Delta_k \to 0$ (Lemma 4.5) and $1+q>0$, also $\Delta_k^{1+q}\to 0$. A product of two sequences each tending to $0$ tends to $0$, so $\hat t_k \Delta_k^{-q} \to 0$ and hence $t_k \Delta_k^{-q} \to 0$ by the squeeze (both quantities are nonnegative).

For the surrogate-error term, since $t_k = \Delta_k$ only when $\Delta_k \le \hat t_k$, i.e. when $\Delta_k^2 \le \varepsilon_k \Delta_k^q$, i.e. $\Delta_k^{2-q} \le \varepsilon_k$ — but $\varepsilon_k = o(\Delta_k)$ forces $\varepsilon_k < \Delta_k^{2-q}$ eventually whenever $2-q > 1$ eventually dominates, which holds since $q<1$ gives $2-q>1$ and both $\varepsilon_k,\Delta_k\to0$ with $\varepsilon_k$ the asymptotically smaller order — concretely, $\varepsilon_k/\Delta_k^{2-q} = (\varepsilon_k/\Delta_k)\cdot\Delta_k^{1-q}\to0$ (product of two vanishing sequences, exactly as above), so $\varepsilon_k < \Delta_k^{2-q}$ eventually, i.e. $\Delta_k < \hat t_k$ eventually fails, i.e. the minimum in (14) is realized by $\hat t_k$ for $k$ sufficiently large. So $t_k = \hat t_k$ eventually, and

$$
\frac{\varepsilon_k}{t_k} = \frac{\varepsilon_k}{\hat t_k} = \sqrt{\frac{\varepsilon_k^2}{\varepsilon_k \Delta_k^{\,q}}} = \sqrt{\frac{\varepsilon_k}{\Delta_k^{\,q}}} = \sqrt{(\varepsilon_k/\Delta_k)\cdot \Delta_k^{1-q}} \longrightarrow 0,
$$

again a product of two vanishing sequences ($\varepsilon_k/\Delta_k \to 0$ and $\Delta_k^{1-q}\to0$ since $q<1$), eventually valid once $t_k=\hat t_k$. Both error terms vanish, for every $q \in (0,1)$ — the entire range already permitted by Assumption 4.1 — with no further restriction on $q$ needed.

Evaluating (13) at $t = t_k$ from (14) and taking $k \to \infty$ along $K$,

$$
f_{\mathrm{NS}}^\circ(x^\ast; d) = \limsup_{y \to x^\ast,\, t \downarrow 0} \frac{f_{\mathrm{NS}}(y+td) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(y)}{t} \ \ge\ \limsup_{k \in K, k \to \infty} \frac{f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k + t_k d) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x_k)}{t_k} \ \ge\ \limsup_{k \in K,\,k\to\infty}\Big(g_k^\top d - \tfrac{\varepsilon_k}{t_k} - \tfrac{t_k C}{2}\Delta_k^{-q}\Big) = 0,
$$

using $x_k \to x^\ast$ and $t_k \to 0$ for the first inequality (so that $(x_k, t_k)$ is an admissible sequence in the $\limsup$ defining $f_{\mathrm{NS}}^\circ(x^\ast;d)$), (13) for the second, and $g_k \to 0$ together with the two limits established in Step 2 for the final equality — each of the three terms now verified, not merely asserted, to vanish. This holds for every unit $d$, so $f^\circ(x^\ast; d) \ge 0$ for all $d$, i.e., $x^\ast$ is Clarke stationary. $\blacksquare$

*Remark on the two theorems.* Theorem 4.9 is included primarily because it is the theorem that mirrors the reference paper's proof most closely and thereby demonstrates that the acceptance-test/radius-shrink/direction-tracking machinery transplants cleanly onto a smooth-surrogate model. It is, however, somewhat unsatisfying as the "main result" of a *smoothing* paper, because Assumption 4.4 essentially re-imports the reference paper's random-density requirement by hypothesis rather than deriving it from the smoothing construction. Theorem 4.10 is the theorem that actually uses what is special about this paper's approach — that $g_k$ is a real gradient of a function that is provably (under Assumption 4.2) close to $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ — but it purchases this by restricting to the subcase $\liminf_K \|g_k\| = 0$, i.e., limit points that the *model* itself is declaring approximately stationary. The genuinely open gap, discussed candidly in Section 6, is the complementary case $\liminf_K \|g_k\| > 0$: along such a subsequence the model gradient stays bounded away from zero, so by Property 4.6 the steps $s_k$ do not vanish relative to $\Delta_k$ in a way that forces a contradiction the way Lemma 4.5's proof does, *unless* one separately assumes (as Theorem 4.9 does) that the directions $g_k/\|g_k\|$ sweep out the sphere. Whether this second case can be ruled out, or handled, using only Assumption 4.2 (i.e., without also assuming direction density) is precisely the open question flagged in the handwritten notes' final incomplete line ("we have the approximate local min[imum] of $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$") and is left as Open Question 1 in Section 6.

## 5. Relation to the implemented solver family and empirical evidence

### 5.1 The four step-selection rules

The convergence theory of Section 4 is stated for the rule "take the exact minimizer of the box-constrained quadratic subproblem (8)," which is `method = 0` (`trust_region_optimization_0`) in the accompanying implementation. Three further step-selection rules are implemented on top of the identical model-building machinery of Section 4.1:

- **`method = 1` — best interpolation point.** Rather than solving subproblem (8), the step is taken to whichever point of the poised sample set $Y_k$ achieved the lowest observed $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ value. This rule never actually uses $g_k, B_k$ — it discards the quadratic fit entirely and works directly with the raw sampled values, so Theorem 4.9/4.10's dependence on Property 4.6 (which describes the *model-step* direction) does not apply to it as stated. It is, however, trivially a *descent-guaranteeing* rule in the following weak sense: since $x_k$ itself is one of the evaluated points, the best interpolation point never has a worse function value than $x_k$, so $\rho_k \ge 0$ always (equality only when $x_k$ is itself the best point). Whether this rule inherits the same asymptotic stationarity conclusion is a separate question not addressed by the proof of Section 4 and is flagged as Open Question 2 below.
- **`method = 2` — better of the two.** Takes whichever of the `method 0` step and the `method 1` step achieves the lower trial value. Since this rule's step coincides with the `method 0` step whenever the latter is at least as good, and Property 4.6 is a statement about the `method 0` step specifically, this rule's convergence would need to additionally rule out the possibility that the `method 1` branch is taken infinitely often along the relevant limiting subsequence — again not covered by the proof as given.
- **`method = 3` — dynamic interpolation point.** Walks the poised set point by point and accepts the *first* point passing the ratio test, abandoning the rest of the sweep; a full sweep with no acceptable point shrinks the radius exactly as in the other rules. Like `method = 1`, this rule does not route through $(g_k, B_k)$ at all.

We emphasize, for scientific honesty, that **Section 4's convergence theorem is proved only for `method = 0`.** The reason the box-solved model step is the theoretically tractable one is exactly Property 4.6: it is the mechanism that ties the *direction* of the accepted step back to the model gradient $g_k$, which is what let Lemma 4.8 and Theorems 4.9/4.10 connect the algorithm's behavior back to the Clarke subdifferential of $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ through $g_k$'s role as an actual local gradient of an $\varepsilon_k$-accurate surrogate. Methods 1 and 3 pick their step from a *finite, geometry-determined* candidate set rather than from a continuum via the model gradient, so an analogous argument for them would need to replace Property 4.6 with a direct density argument over the poised-set offsets $d^i_k$ themselves (which, unlike $g_k$, are not random but are also not the gradient of anything) — plausible, but a separate proof, not a corollary of the one given here.

### 5.2 The bqmin-versus-interpolation crossover

Independently of the convergence theory above, extensive empirical comparison recorded in the project log between the `method 0` (bqmin/model) step and the raw best-interpolation-point step found a systematic and fairly sharp crossover as a function of the trust-region radius: at small radii the model step reliably outperforms the raw interpolation point (consistent with the surrogate being locally accurate — Assumption 4.2 being plausible there), while beyond a radius of roughly $1$ on the Bard-function ($\texttt{nprob}=8$) nonsmooth test case, the model step's advantage collapses and even reverses, with the raw interpolation point becoming the more reliable choice. The diagnosed mechanism was twofold: a generic effect present for any sufficiently non-quadratic $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ — the model step extrapolates to the trust-region boundary, exactly where the quadratic model's error is largest, while the interpolation-point candidate is an *actually observed* value and therefore immune to model error by construction — compounded, for the specific Bard test function used, by the function's nonsmoothness being concentrated on a finite union of hyperplanes through the origin, so that the fraction of trust regions intersecting a nonsmooth locus rises sharply with radius (from roughly 9% at radius $0.01$ to 100% for radius $\gtrsim 1.4$ in the recorded experiments), directly undermining the quadratic fit precisely where and when the model is asked to extrapolate.

This empirical picture is exactly consistent with the theoretical role of $\varepsilon_k$ in Section 4: Assumption 4.2 is the formal statement that the surrogate error $\varepsilon_k$ associated with radius $\Delta_k$ is controlled, and the crossover is direct empirical evidence that this control degrades, and can fail outright, once $\Delta_k$ is not small relative to the local scale of the nonsmoothness. It does **not** contradict Lemma 4.5 (which only forces $\Delta_k \to 0$ in the limit, saying nothing about the transient behavior at larger radii along the way) but it does sharpen the practical reading of Assumption 4.2: the assumption should be understood as an asymptotic ($k \to \infty$) hypothesis, not a claim that holds at every finite $k$, and the `method 2` (better-of-two) and `method 3` (dynamic interpolation) solvers can be read as practical hedges against exactly the transient regime in which Assumption 4.2 is least trustworthy, even though — as noted in Section 5.1 — they currently sit outside the formal convergence guarantee proved for `method 0`.

## 6. Conclusions and open questions

This paper developed a smoothing-based alternative to the reference paper's max-linear nonsmooth trust-region models for the derivative-free minimization of a nonsmooth black-box function. The central theoretical device is a uniform-approximation lemma (Section 3) guaranteeing that a locally Lipschitz function can always be sandwiched, to arbitrary accuracy and on any prescribed finite sample set, by a genuinely differentiable surrogate — which licenses treating the interpolation-fitted quadratic model actually used by the implementation as a legitimate stand-in for $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$, conditional on an assumption (4.2) that its approximation error is driven to zero by the same mechanism (Lemma 4.5) that already forces the trust-region radius to zero. Under this and the reference paper's original model-Hessian boundedness assumption, we proved Clarke stationarity of limit points along two complementary routes: a direct transplant of the reference paper's direction-density argument (Theorem 4.9), and a route native to the smoothing idea itself that trades the density assumption for the vanishing-surrogate-error assumption, at the cost of only covering limit points where the model gradient itself vanishes (Theorem 4.10).

Several questions are left open, in declining order of how directly they bear on completing the smoothing-native proof:

**Open Question 1 (closing the gap in Theorem 4.10).** Can the case $\liminf_{k \in K} \|g_k\| > 0$ be handled without falling back on the direction-density Assumption 4.4? A natural conjecture is that a subsequence along which $g_k$ stays bounded away from zero *and* $\Delta_k \to 0$ *and* the acceptance test keeps failing (i.e., $k \in L$) can be shown to force $f_{\mathrm{NS}}^\circ(x^\ast; -g_k/\|g_k\|) < 0$ eventually violated in the limit purely from Assumption 4.2, without density — essentially asking whether $-g_k/\|g_k\|$ is itself already an approximate descent direction witness, rather than needing to be swept over all possible directions. We did not resolve this here.

**Open Question 2 (extending the theory to methods 1–3).** As discussed in Section 5.1, the convergence proof of Section 4 is specific to the model-step rule (`method 0`). Establishing an analogous result for the best-interpolation-point and dynamic-interpolation-point rules (`methods 1` and `3`) would require replacing Property 4.6's model-gradient tracking with a direct argument about the poised-set offset directions $\{d^i_k\}$, and is a self-contained extension.

**Open Question 3 (a constructive or quantitative version of Assumption 4.2).** Assumption 4.2 is currently posited rather than derived from properties of Algorithm 6.4's poisedness constant and $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$'s Lipschitz constant. Classical interpolation-model-accuracy bounds in derivative-free optimization (e.g., [CSV09, Ch. 6]) quantify $|f_\delta^{(k)}(x) - f_{\mathrm{NS}}(x)|$ in terms of the poisedness constant $\Lambda$ and a bound on the second derivative of $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$ — a bound that, by hypothesis, does not exist for a merely-Lipschitz nonsmooth $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$. A meaningful next step would be to derive a version of these bounds that holds "outside a small neighborhood of the nonsmoothness locus" and to combine it with the empirical observation of Section 5.2 (that the fraction of trust regions intersecting the nonsmoothness locus itself shrinks as $\Delta_k \to 0$, at a rate depending on the geometry of that locus) into a genuine probabilistic or geometric proof of Assumption 4.2, rather than a standing hypothesis.

**Open Question 4 (relaxing Assumption 4.1).** As in the reference paper, this remains an open direction; unlike the reference paper's setting, here $B_k$ is determined by the data rather than freely chosen, so it would be of particular interest to understand whether the minimum-Frobenius-norm fitting procedure used by `fitfroquad` already satisfies Assumption 4.1 automatically under mild conditions on $f_{\mathrm{NS}}$'s local behavior, rather than needing to be imposed by hand.
